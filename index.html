Why and when do Students change Majors?

Introduction

This is a contribution to the IEEE VGTC VPG Data-Visualization Contest. The goal of the contest is to understand perceived vs. actual student interest in majors and to understand whether students that are determined a good fit for their major stay in it more commonly than those who are not.

Data

The data contains information about planned majors (in high school), actual majors declared in the first and second year of college, scores that capture the fit of the student to the major, and a general ability score, in addition to gender.

Visualization

Our visualization is a combination of Parallel Sets and Mosaic Plots. The User can choose to highlight a major, which reveals the flow of students across the years, with intermediate blocks showing whether they stay or leave. We aggregated the majors into subject areas, to reduce visual clutter. The flow of students that remains within the major is highlighted in blue, the leaving students are highlighted in red.

In addition to the flow of students, the individual blocks in the parallel sets are partitioned, so that we can see the distribution of students by gender and by ACT's Interest-Major Fit Index, a score estimating the fit of a student to a subject area. On the far right, a column on ACT Composite scores shows general academic ability.

Highlights

Here we highlight two particularly interesting cases.

Health Sci. & Techno. has the maximum change of students between planned major and the first year, with more than half changing the subject. While students in all major-fit category leave the subject, we see a slightly larger proportion of students with bad fit leave the major. The main benefactor in the first year is science and math, with more than half of those changing their minds going into this area.

Arts: Visual & Performing are interesting because students typically fit well according to the interest-major fit index, and it also sees very little outflux over the years. We can also see that the major is slightly female dominated.
